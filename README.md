| 1988–1993 | Consolidated Press Holdings / Chemplex Australia | Petrochemical operations |

## The Three Phenol Plants

A notable feature of this site was that **three entirely different phenol production technologies** were operated over 25 years on the same footprint:

| Plant | Dept. | Period | Technology | Outcome |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Sulphonation Plant | #223 | 1941–1966 | Batch sulphonation (Monsanto Krummrich process) | Converted to continuous in 1957; eventually replaced |
| SD Cyclohexane Plant | #77 | 1964–1968 | Scientific Design catalytic oxidation of cyclohexane | Failed — never exceeded 60% design capacity; later repurposed for BTX rail tanker handling |
| Cumene-Phenol Plant | #87 | 1968–~1998 | Lummus cumene process | Successful; Royal Australian Chemical Institute Plant of the Year 1968; shut down due to import competition |

## Key Chemical Processes on Site

### Styrene Monomer Production (commissioned 1977)
Ethane → **Ethylene** → (+ Benzene) → **Ethylbenzene** → **Styrene Monomer**

**Supporting processes:** BTX purification (Litol plant), Alkylation, Dehydrogenation.

### Cumene-Phenol Production (Dept. 87)
Benzene + Propylene → **Cumene** → (oxidation) → **Cumene Hydroperoxide (CHP)** → (cleavage) → **Phenol + Acetone**

## The Surviving Building — Identification

Based on aerial imagery from 1974, 1982, 2010, and 2012, a structure in the east-central portion of the site was tracked through demolition. Key findings:

- **1974:** Area undeveloped.
- **1982:** Six tanks/silos appear — consistent with the 4 oxidation reactors added progressively to Dept. 87, plus associated separator vessels. The fourth oxidiser was added ~1981.
- **2010:** Full structure still standing, post-plant shutdown (2005 for Cumene-Phenol, 2009 for site).
- **2012:** All surrounding infrastructure demolished; **one shed remains** — consistent with Wikipedia's specific note: *"completely dismantled except for the old control room"*.

The interior of the surviving building shows large-diameter overhead pipework, a mezzanine gantry level, and an industrial open floor plan — more consistent with a **process utility hall or pump hall** than the control room itself. The actual control room was likely the enclosed upper level accessed by the visible internal staircase, a typical 1960s petrochemical layout.

## Environmental Observations

### The Dark Oil Spill (~3x3m)

**Location:** Adjacent to where the oxidation reactor vessels (silos) stood next to the doof warehouse
**Characteristics:** Dark colour, petroleum/petrol odour.

**Most Likely Composition (ranked by probability):**
1. **Heavy ends tar** — dark, viscous distillation byproduct explicitly documented on site; piped to boiler house as fuel; any line or vessel failure would produce exactly this stain.
2. **Cumene hydroperoxide decomposition residue** — CHP breakdown produces dark tarry fractions; the oxidisers were directly adjacent.
3. **BTX mixture** — if the building was repurposed for BTX handling (Dept. 77 history), a petroleum-smelling dark spill is consistent.
4. **Transformer/hydraulic oil with PCBs** — dark, petroleum-smelling, and extraordinarily persistent.

### The Rain-Triggered Sulphur Smell

**Observation:** Strong sulphur smell lasting ~10 minutes after rain begins, then dissipating.

**Mechanism:** Rainwater percolating into contaminated soil displaces trapped volatile sulfur compounds upward through soil air pockets — a concentrated burst that ceases once the soil becomes saturated.

**Likely Sources:**
- **H₂S absorbed into soil** from Ethylene plant operations (dedicated incinerator on site confirms significant H₂S volumes).
- **DMDS residues** from sulfiding agent use — extremely persistent, intense sulphur odour.
- **Sulfate mineral hydrolysis** — sulfate-laden heavy ends tar was burned in boiler #4; the resulting ash and particulates settled across the site and react with rainwater to release SO₂.

*The sulphur smell and the dark oil spill are likely **connected** — both pointing to the heavy ends tar waste stream as a common source.*

## Field Testing & Analysis

### DIY Indicators

| Test | Method | Interpretation |
|---|---|---|
| **Smell profile** | Direct observation | Petroleum/petrol = BTX/cumene; sweet petroleum = styrene/cumene; medicinal/antiseptic = phenol component |
| **Visual sheen** | Wet surface observation | Rainbow iridescence = petroleum hydrocarbon; matte black = heavy tar or polymerised styrene |
| **Water contact** | Drip water on spill edge | Beading + sheen = hydrocarbon; cloudy white dispersion = phenol (water-miscible) |
| **pH strips** | Test soil at spill edge | pH <4 = acid residue (sulfuric acid); normal soil = pH 6–7 |

**Sample collection protocol:** Use a glass jar with a metal lid, filled with no air gap. Collect from 2–5cm depth using nitrile gloves. Keep refrigerated and deliver to the lab within 48 hours.

## Critical Health Advisory

> [!CAUTION]
> **This site presents multiple serious, concurrent health hazards.**

| Hazard | Why It Is Particularly Dangerous Here |
|---|---|
| **Benzene** | No safe exposure level; confirmed leukaemia cause; odour threshold unreliable as warning. |
| **Phenol** | Absorbs through intact skin with **no pain sensation** — exposure is not self-evident. |
| **Mercury vapour** | Completely odourless and invisible; neurological damage is irreversible. |
| **H₂S** | Causes olfactory fatigue rapidly — you lose the ability to smell it while still being actively poisoned. |
| **Asbestos** | Highly probable in 1941–1970s pipework insulation; fibres become airborne when disturbed. |
| **PCBs** | Absorb through skin; accumulate in body tissue over time. |

**Anyone spending extended time in this building should be aware that multiple carcinogens, neurotoxins, and systemic poisons are likely present in dust, soil, residual liquids, and potentially the air. Professional environmental assessment would be required before any formal occupation or use of the space.**
